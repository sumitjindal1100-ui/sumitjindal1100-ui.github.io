01 / About

From a computer science and business degree to analysis work I actually enjoy.

I studied computer science and business at Saint Mary's University and spent a few years in front-line and support roles, where I kept gravitating to the same thing: figuring out why a process was slow, pulling the numbers to prove it, and writing down a fix the team could follow.

These days I work in IT and clinical systems support at Nova Scotia Health, and I am moving deliberately toward Business and Systems Analyst work. To show what that looks like rather than just claim it, I built seven projects end to end: real datasets queried in SQL, interactive dashboards, BPMN process maps with as-is and to-be states, and business requirements documents that tie every change back to a measured finding.

I like the analyst job because it sits between people and systems. You have to listen well, ask the awkward question, and then leave behind something concrete: a number, a diagram, a spec. That is the work in the cards below, and most of it is live and clickable.

Healthcare Patient Intake

Why does one in five patients miss a primary-care appointment? Ten SQL queries on 110,527 real appointment records find the causes (long booking horizons, a single late SMS, a tiny frequent-no-show cohort), and a redesigned digital-front-door intake answers each one. Every BPMN element traces back to a specific SQL finding, with a projected drop from 20.2% to 13.5%.

Product thinking · hybrid app

Public Benefits Navigator

A screening tool that tells people which benefits they likely qualify for (SNAP, Medicaid, CHIP, WIC, LIHEAP, EITC). The design decision is the point: a deterministic, fully tested Python rules engine makes every eligibility call, and the language model only parses free-text intake and explains the result. It never gets to decide. The rules suite is a hard CI gate that already caught two of my own wrong thresholds.
